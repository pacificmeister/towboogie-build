What is a Tow Boogie?

A tow boogie is a small, motorized watercraft that tows a rider up to foiling speed — typically 8 to 12 mph — then releases them to ride waves, ocean swells, or downwind bumps on an unpowered prone foil board.

Think of it as a personal, pocket-sized jet ski. It's electric, quiet, compact enough to carry under one arm, and exists for one purpose: getting you up on foil without paddling, without a boat, and without a kite.

Once you're on foil and riding energy (a wave, a bump, a swell), you let go of the tow rope. The tow boogie idles or returns to shore while you ride. That's the magic — pure, unpowered foiling with a motorized launch.

🎯 The One-Sentence Version: A tow boogie gets you foiling. Gravity and ocean energy keep you foiling. No motor under your board, no noise, no drag — just you and the wave.

How Does a Tow Boogie Work?

The basic concept is simple:

  1. Launch: You're lying or standing on your prone foil board in the water, holding a tow rope connected to the boogie.
  2. Tow-up: The boogie pulls you forward. At 8-12 mph, your foil generates enough lift to rise out of the water.
  3. Release: Once you're on foil and riding a wave or bump, you drop the rope.
  4. Ride: You surf the wave, pump the foil, link bumps — pure foiling, no motor.
  5. Reset: The boogie circles back (or a buddy drives it) and you go again.

Key Components

Every tow boogie — DIY or commercial — has the same core components:

  • Hull/Body: A buoyant platform, often a modified bodyboard or custom fiberglass shell
  • Motor: A brushless DC motor (typically 3-8 kW) with a propeller or jet drive
  • Battery: Lithium-ion or LiFePO4 pack (typically 1-3 kWh)
  • ESC: Electronic speed controller — the brain that manages power delivery
  • Remote: Waterproof handheld controller (the rider or a buddy operates it)
  • Tow Point: Where the rope attaches — positioning matters for stability
💡 Pro Tip: The tow point should be near the nose, angled slightly upward (~5°). This prevents the boogie from nosediving under load. Builders on FOIL.zone discovered this through hundreds of iterations.

Tow Boogie vs eFoil — What's the Difference?

People often confuse tow boogies with eFoils. They're fundamentally different:

🚀 Tow Boogie

  • Motor is on a separate device
  • You ride an unpowered foil board
  • Natural wave-riding feel
  • No motor drag under board
  • Lighter board = more agile
  • Can ride any prone foil setup

⚡ eFoil

  • Motor is on the foil mast
  • Board + motor are one unit
  • Self-powered cruising
  • Motor creates drag when off
  • Heavier board (20-30 lbs more)
  • Works on flat water

The key insight: an eFoil is a self-contained ride — you cruise on flat water under motor power. A tow boogie is a launch assist — it gets you up, then you ride nature's energy. For wave riders and downwind enthusiasts, the tow boogie delivers a far more natural experience.

🏄 The wave rider's choice: Most experienced foilers who've tried both prefer the tow boogie for wave riding. No prop wash, no motor weight, just a clean foil glide. The eFoil is better for flat-water cruising and recreational riding.

Who Uses Tow Boogies?

Tow boogies appeal to several groups:

  • Prone foil surfers — Skip the exhausting paddle-in and catch more waves per session
  • Downwind foilers — Get up on foil in light wind or flat spots between bumps
  • Wing foilers — Launch assist in light wind days
  • Aging athletes / injury recovery — Foil without the physical demands of paddling into waves
  • Coaches — Tow students up reliably for training
  • Remote/offshore riders — Access outer reefs and offshore breaks without a boat

The common thread: these are people who already know how to foil and want a better way to get launched. A tow boogie isn't a beginner device — it's a tool for experienced riders to maximize ride time.

Types of Tow Boogies

1. Self-Tow (Solo Operation)

You hold the remote in one hand and the tow rope in the other. Once up on foil, you release both and ride. This is the most common setup and works well in open water with consistent swell.

2. Buddy-Tow (Operated by Partner)

A friend on shore or in the water operates the boogie via remote while you focus on riding. This is ideal for wave riding — the operator can position the tow for optimal wave entry.

3. Autonomous / GPS-Return

Some advanced builds include GPS waypoint return, so the boogie drives itself back to a starting point after you release. This is emerging tech — only a few DIY builders have implemented it.

Hull Types

  • Bodyboard conversion — Cheapest, easiest to build, most popular for DIY
  • Custom fiberglass shell — Better hydrodynamics, used by commercial brands
  • Inflatable — Portable, but less stable and slower
  • Hard-shell with jet drive — Premium commercial option (e.g., Zero Tow)

Can You Build Your Own?

Absolutely. The DIY tow boogie community is thriving, centered on FOIL.zone — the world's largest DIY efoil and foiling forum with 5,300+ members and 135,000+ posts.

A typical DIY build involves:

  1. Choosing your hull — Most builders start with a bodyboard ($30-60)
  2. Selecting motor + prop — Brushless outrunner, 3-6 kW, with a matched propeller
  3. Picking a battery — 12S or 14S lithium pack, 1-2 kWh
  4. Getting the electronics — ESC, waterproof remote, wiring, connectors
  5. Assembly — Motor mount, waterproofing, tow point, rope attachment

The whole build takes 2-4 days and requires basic tools (drill, soldering iron, heat shrink). No CNC, no 3D printing, no composites work needed for V1.

Frequently Asked Questions

How fast does a tow boogie go?

Most tow boogies reach 10-15 mph (16-24 km/h). You only need 8-12 mph to get a foil rider up on plane. Commercial units like the Zero Tow can exceed 20 mph, but that's overkill for most riders.

How long does the battery last?

A typical session gets 15-30 tow-ups on a single charge, depending on battery size and conditions. With a 1.5 kWh battery, expect 30-45 minutes of active tow time. Since you're only using the motor for the initial pull (not continuous riding), the battery lasts much longer than an eFoil.

Is it legal?

Regulations vary by location. In most places, small electric watercraft under a certain size/power don't require registration. Check your local maritime authority. The small size and electric motor generally keep tow boogies below regulatory thresholds.

Can I use it in flat water?

Yes, but it's less fun. In flat water, you'd need the boogie towing you continuously (like an eFoil but with a rope). The real magic is in waves and bumps where you release and ride free. For pure flat-water riding, an eFoil is a better choice.

How portable is a tow boogie?

Very. A typical build weighs 15-25 lbs (7-12 kg) and is about the size of a bodyboard. Commercial units like the Zero Tow weigh around 20 kg. You can carry it under one arm and fit it in any car.

What foil board do I use with it?

Any prone foil board works. Most riders use boards in the 4'0" to 5'0" range. The beauty of a tow boogie is that your board stays light and clean — no motor mount, no battery compartment, no wires. Just a regular foil board.
